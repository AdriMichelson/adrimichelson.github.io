So I don't want to share this actively. Instead, I'm just going to log these here along with things I'm more interested in sharing. If you see it and are interested, you see it. I do better when I externalize what is going on to mark it for what it is and see how to make things better.  There are opportunities to genuinely connect when being open and vulnerable, even in the polarizing mindwarp of social media/politics/life. I just don't have the desire to smack it in people's faces/feeds/etc. Just a little pocket to deposit thoughts.

I think the biggest mental hurdle I'm doing right now is negotiating the idea of how familiar things can be versus what it will be on chemotherapy. My family and I don't know what my reaction to it will be; however, from their interaction in oncology over 60 years combined and my oncologist, the first time, I should at least treat it like I will react poorly to it. I hate that I have to do that, but for my safety, I guess I should. The thought of going through this in a pandemic where my parents have to travel up to help is a continuously, incredibly scary nightmare. COVID19 and the probability of infection always have bothered me.  The absolute drama would be for my parents to get sick trying to help me, their cancer sick daughter- that could be a killer soap with an excellent all-star casting—Oscar-worthy.

I think I'm accepting I'm going to be a dried-up raisin on this chemo preemptively. I still can workout, eat well, and do things- in fact- I very much now want to do that more than ever, being a balloon for most of this year and coughing and constantly feeling sick and inconsistently able to do so. I'm experiencing weird behavior on steroids pre-chemo (on steroids to handle the onset of nephrotic syndrome that caused me to blow up twice my size). Being on my feet for 40 minutes to wait in a line to vote absolutely killed my energy; still, I felt the kind of full in my soul you get if you found the perfect bowl of spicy soup in a new part of town and are trying to mop it up with a piece of crusty bread. There's a lightness that I've not had in ages, and it's incredibly grounding and reassuring. 

Maybe from the outside, you hear yourself saying, "Adrienne, you're pushing it," but god guys, I'd instead push it, then act as if I ever want to be strapped to an ICU  bed with a tube breathing for me ever again. The hard part will just be the negotiation of my limits and putting my ego away. I just know I will get mad if people choose to negotiate my capabilities away from me, so if you're reading this, for christ sake don't play that game with me. Just give me space, lead me with a carrot- I'll be sure to carry my own stick.

This cancer stuff, being strapped down to a surgical ICU bed with tubes hanging out of you, all of that melts away the nonsense you've put on yourself because you realize how much of it doesn't matter. So many things just simply do not matter. Having worked in politics for seven years now, all the pettiness, all the small things I fretted over, is slowly melting away. And with that, I guess it's made my desire to just try a lot more salient. Maybe starting a company that makes things better and advocating for better, more affordable healthcare access for people worse off than me here in Nevada. So I shall and try to do those things while doing this chemotherapy thing, and I may journal it here or not- I don't know. 

Just bear with me in the meantime.

-A
