Wow, okay, this last month, I learned a lot. You know when you are given advice but have to experience the thing to implement it fully? That was me this past month. As I mentioned last time, there was just a lot of uncertainty about how I would react to my first round of chemotherapy and what I could do in terms of my mind, body, and how my spirit would hold up. I wrote in my first blog about my expectations and negotiated what I wanted to do. Well, for this first round, it was quite a ride on all three fronts.

So far, the process is more demanding, magnified by our elections when there is a very contagious virus attacking the very things I had surgery to correct (kidneys, lungs, heart). Watching political leaders wait until hospitals are overwhelmed to do the correct thing, I want to be my own political advocate, but feeling like I am being ignored is incredibly hard. I know it's not intentional, but that's the byproduct of how things are running and probably why I don't ever see myself working in government. It's been a very fighty response to not wanting to live and battle cancer in a perpetual bubble alone for the next few months like a crazy hypochondriac.

But, I have decided to back off. A friend of mine from college wrote to me recently, and this stuck out:

"I watched war movies as a kid, I always wondered why everyone didn't play dead. Your sense of mission and sacrifice have helped me understand the type of people who will give anything for the people and causes that matter to them. So please give me this: your commitment to put yourself first and prioritize the self-care, rest, and recovery you need right now. This political season will be wild and strenuous, and I and so many others are prepared to fight on your behalf when and if you need a break."

This was a couple of weeks ago, but I've been sitting with it and thinking about it. Over the last month, as I prepped for chemotherapy, I went to all my specialists, did my first chemotherapy, dealt with the side effects, had pain- lots of pain, and learned to manage it.

Mentally and spiritually, I oscillated a lot, and that was incredibly hard. This is the first time in my adult life, I actively and consistently want my parents to help both professionally and their ascribed duties, but due to COVID19, it's all tele-parenting. As someone who works with data frequently to then "does things" in politics/startups, I have been trying to find a tool or platform that holistically helps to manage information and guide me in the right direction for this journey.

For me, this is important because in this moment of crisis in our country where things are so in flux, if I can do everything I can to prevent myself from having emergent symptoms that force me to be hospitalized, then that is what I need to do. That means managing all my information in a way that gives me ideas on where to go and gives me the tools to advocate for myself when I interact with doctors and nurses. Sometimes so many things are going on at once that we can't correctly identify problems within ourselves; first, it needs to be painted on a page or come to a critical moment when it's right in front of us, forcing us to make a choice. That's the whole point of having data dashboards, managing all the information, prioritizing, drawing actionable insights. As someone fighting cancer, I need to learn more about how I react to chemotherapy and do better in response.

 In that spirit and in my research, I've found a combination of apps/tools/resources, but the problem is a coordination problem and gaps in what data I'm looking at. I only have so much energy for upkeep. This is why my parents have been great. We talk every morning and night to check in on my symptoms. The catch here is that combined, my parents have over 60+ years of experience in Emergency Healthcare and Internal Healthcare on three separate continents. Yes, I am a lucky duck in having them on speed dial. Still, they're not in my meetings with doctors, not nutritional specialists, not physical therapists. While we praise them and are fantastic, medical doctors are indeed limited in their capabilities and knowledge. I strongly believe in categorizing things into "mind, spirit, body" paradigm. Doctors can cover and specialize in parts of the body, but then there are other areas.

Still, I crave something to help me ground myself as I go through this experience. To my friend's quote earlier, maybe I wouldn't use the word self-care but substitute it with "infrastructure." A very political word, but I think it sounds more grounded and consistent and can incorporate the whole "101 self care techniques" pinterest ideas. The point is to have a holistic framework.

I am missing information-wise as a cancer fighter, a holistic framework to watch my journey to plug in my information and paint a picture and see where I can do better. In research, there are many cool innovations in cancer-fighting- the problem is these products, tools, data, techniques are all targeted for an audience of scientists and researchers. That's great! Tool wise I've found no system for a person diagnosed with cancer can open up, plugin information, and have it as a tool for reference.

 Healthcare itself is a weird, messy thing in America with many obstacles, middlemen, coordination problems. This last month I could recount 12 stories of this being a problem forcing me to stop and rectify or use my keen sense of political organizing to fix something. I really have to budget my energy; otherwise, I need to rely on others, which in normal times, yes! I want to rely on my community of friends and those willing to help. But pandemic! Ugh. The conundrum of my journey.

So I think I will create a system to keep track of vitals, food, log mood, etc. into a private GitHub project that I can potentially open-source and invite other people who have had family or friends go through this contribute. Obviously iterate, start small with a spreadsheet, maybe put it into R/shiny thing, perhaps something else. Maybe I/we/others can build an open-source framework to help those doing the most in upkeep.

    Even as 2020 has brought us all challenges, exposed our limits, cracks, and weaknesses- it's an opportunity to step back and think about how we can do better and do better together. I hope to do this project in the next month and say, "yes, this helps, and things are getting easier to manage," when so much is up in the air. It gives me solace and something to look forward to-, and I think that's all I can ask for.

Chemo round two is on Monday to see if I have more intense symptoms or if things improve. I hope it's the latter.
